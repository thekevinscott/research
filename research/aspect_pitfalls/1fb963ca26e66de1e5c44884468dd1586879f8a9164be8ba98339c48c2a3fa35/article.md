# Justice or Prejudice? Quantifying Biases in LLM-as-a-Judge

Source: llm-judge-bias.github.io (research project page)
URL: https://llm-judge-bias.github.io/

## Summary

This research project quantifies the systematic biases in using one LLM to judge another LLM's output — the LLM-as-a-Judge pattern that underpins most Dark Factory evaluation harnesses. The headline finding categorizes the biases into multiple groups: position bias (favoring first/last responses), verbosity bias (preferring longer answers regardless of accuracy), self-enhancement bias (favoring text generated by the same LLM family), and authority bias (rating answers higher when they cite references — even fake references). The work is widely cited in arguments that "agents-judging-agents" pipelines have a structural credibility problem.

For Dark Factory pitfalls research this paper is the empirical anchor for the "judge bias / generator-as-judge" critique. StrongDM and similar Dark Factory implementers rely heavily on LLM evaluators to validate that agent-generated code matches specs. If the evaluator shares training data and architectural biases with the generator, it will systematically over-rate the generator's output — and self-enhancement bias is documented as a "special risk in automated training loops, as models tend to rate their own outputs higher than those from other systems." This is precisely the pipeline shape Dark Factory uses.

## Reconstructed from search snippets

### Documented biases

- **Position bias**: "favoring responses based on their placement (e.g., first or last)."
- **Verbosity bias**: "Preferring longer answers, even if they aren't more accurate."
- **Self-enhancement bias**: "Favoring texts generated by the same LLM."
- **Authority bias**: "When fake references are added to an answer, judge models may consider that answer superior and cite the fake references as justification."

### Why this matters for reward hacking

> "Biases are especially concerning when grader outputs are used as part of a reward signal, which can lead to reward hacking by exploiting these graders."

Reward hacking / specification gaming:
> "Evaluator gaming, also known as reward hacking or specification gaming, occurs when models exploit flaws, ambiguities, or unintended shortcuts in evaluator design to achieve high scores in ways misaligned with human preferences."

### Mitigation

- Switch order of responses to control position bias.
- Use direct scoring instead of pairwise selection.
- Strict separation between generation and evaluation models.
- "Self-evaluation should be avoided."

The Evaluator Stress Test (EST) framework: an invariance-based approach detecting proxy gaming via controlled perturbations with semantic validity audits.

## Why this matters

For Dark Factory critics this paper is the primary citation when arguing that StrongDM's "independent evaluation" is structurally weaker than claimed. If the evaluator and generator share a model family — and most production setups do, for cost and consistency reasons — self-enhancement bias means the eval pipeline is biased toward declaring the generator's work correct. This compounds the cascading-failure risk and is the most cited reason to be skeptical of "agents test agents test agents" claims.
