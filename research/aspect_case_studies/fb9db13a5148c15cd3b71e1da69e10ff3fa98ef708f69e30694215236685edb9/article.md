# The dark factory is real, most developers are getting slower, and your org chart is the bottleneck

## What this is

A widely-cited Substack essay that operationalizes Dan Shapiro's 5-level framework, ties it to the StrongDM dark-factory case, and pairs it with field-data on AI productivity. The author writes from the perspective of advising teams trying to climb the levels.

## The 5 levels (as paraphrased from snippets)

- Level 1: spicy autocomplete
- Level 2: AI handles multifile changes, navigates the codebase, builds features spanning multiple modules - while the human still reads all the code. Most teams are stuck here.
- Levels 3-4: Progressive automation; engineers shift from writing code to reviewing and directing AI work.
- Level 5: The Dark Factory - "manufacturing facilities run entirely by robots, dark because there is no need for lights when nobody is inside." A black box that turns specs into software, no human writes or reviews code.

## Direct quotes / near-quotes from snippets

> "Three engineers at StrongDM run a software factory where no human writes code and no human reviews code. The system takes specifications written in markdown, builds the software, tests it against behavioral scenarios, and produces shippable artifacts."

> "In one rigorous randomized controlled trial, experienced open-source developers working in codebases they already knew completed tasks 19% slower when using AI tools. Not faster. Slower."

> "They predicted AI would make them 24% faster, and after the study they still believed it had made them 20% faster. They were wrong about the direction, let alone the magnitude."

> "The frontier teams aren't just using better AI tools. They've rebuilt everything around a fundamentally different workflow - one where the bottleneck has moved from how fast you can write code to how precisely you can describe what should exist."

## Key claims

- The bottleneck is now organisational and specificational, not coding speed.
- Most teams are stuck at Level 2 and getting slower while believing they are getting faster.
- "Most software is not being written for a new product on a greenfield codebase - the overwhelming majority of professional coding involves modifying, debugging, and extending systems with years of accumulated context."
- The post offers "5 prompts to get from level 2 to 5," focused on writing better specs and harness scaffolding.

## Takeaway

This essay is one of the most-cited bridges between the StrongDM case study and a general framework that ordinary engineering leaders can apply.
