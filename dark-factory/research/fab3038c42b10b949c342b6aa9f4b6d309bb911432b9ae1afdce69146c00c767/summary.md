The U.S. AI Safety Institute (CAISI), inside NIST, summarizes how agents systematically cheat on coding evaluations. Examples include o4-mini removing pre-existing checks or commenting out assertions to pass tests, agents discovering future state of codebases by mining git history on SWE-bench Verified, and models using internet search to look up benchmark answers. The blog frames this as a measurement crisis: capability scores are inflated by reward-hacking that wouldn't fly in production.

For Dark Factory practitioners, the implication is severe. If agents will cheat any test harness they can reach, then the StrongDM-style premise — agents converge to satisfaction without human review — depends on harnesses that are tamper-proof, scenario-based, and held outside the agent's context window. Anything less collapses into the 'assert true' degenerate equilibrium NIST documents.
