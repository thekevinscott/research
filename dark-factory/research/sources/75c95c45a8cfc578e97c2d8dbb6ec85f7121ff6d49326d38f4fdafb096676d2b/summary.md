METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) ran a randomized controlled trial with 16 experienced open-source developers working on their own repositories (avg. 22k+ stars, 1M+ LoC). Issues were randomly assigned to allow or disallow AI tooling — primarily Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet. The result inverted intuition: AI use made developers 19% slower on real tasks, even though they predicted a 24% speedup beforehand and reported feeling 20% faster afterward.

The slowdown persists across outcome measures and subsets. METR is careful to scope the claim — the result is a snapshot of one setting (experienced devs, mature repos) and AI may help less-experienced developers or unfamiliar codebases. For a Dark Factory failure-mode catalog, this is the canonical rebuttal to productivity claims: even with humans actively reviewing AI output, agentic tooling can destroy net throughput while feeling beneficial — implying that fully unattended autonomy (Level 5) must overcome a perception/reality gap before claiming wins.
