Kellan Elliott-McCrea — former CTO of Etsy and prior engineering leadership at Flickr — writes from inside his own three-person dark-factory experiment. His skeptic critique is structural rather than technological: code has always been the easy part of building software; the hard parts are coordination, requirements clarity, organizational learning, customer trust, and shared understanding. Removing humans from the code-review loop addresses the easy bottleneck while leaving the hard ones untouched (and arguably worsening them, by removing the moments when humans build shared understanding through review).

He pairs this with explicit discomfort about the AI hype cycle's amoral/sometimes outright evil actors. For Dark Factory failure analysis, this is the most credible inside-the-tent skeptic: a practitioner who has done the work and concluded that the framing is wrong, not just that the tooling is immature. His implicit prediction is that Dark Factory teams will succeed on coding throughput and fail on the metrics that actually move businesses.
