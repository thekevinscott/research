Simon Willison's link-blog entry on the Tea app breach quotes the company's official statement and notes important nuance: although the breach is widely cited as a 'vibe coding' failure, the underlying code predates the vibe-coding era. The Tea app exposed 72,000 selfies and 13,000+ government IDs through a misconfigured Firebase legacy storage bucket; a second breach exposed 1.1M private messages via broken API authorization.

Why this still matters for Dark Factory failure analysis: the failure modes (default-misconfigured storage, missing IDOR checks, no auth on chat APIs) are exactly the failure modes that LLM-generated code reproduces at population scale today (see CSA, Lovable, Base44 entries above). The Tea breach is the pre-vibe-coding-era version of what the vibe-coded apps now ship by default. Willison's careful framing — 'the codebase predates February 2024' — underscores how easy it is to mis-attribute breaches to AI when the real constant is human inattention to security defaults.
